The quiet art of restraint — why less is rarely easy

Alex Morgan · March 2, 2025 · 6 min read

There's a particular kind of discipline that doesn't get talked about enough in design circles: the discipline of not adding. Of looking at something that works and resisting every instinct to embellish it, to prove your worth through addition.

Every designer I've admired deeply has had this quality. Not minimalism as an aesthetic religion, but restraint as a working practice — a constant, quiet negotiation between what could be there and what should be.

The addition bias

There's a well-documented cognitive tendency called the addition bias: when asked to improve something, people almost universally think first about what to add, rarely about what to remove. Researchers at the University of Virginia demonstrated this by giving participants a Lego structure and asking them to improve it. The vast majority added bricks. Almost no one removed them — even when removing a single block was the cleaner, more structurally sound solution.

Design suffers from the same bias, and it's compounded by the need to feel useful. If a designer spends a sprint removing three things instead of building five new ones, it can feel like they haven't done enough. The removal is invisible. The additions are a portfolio piece.

Perfection is achieved not when there is nothing more to add, but when there is nothing left to take away.

Antoine de Saint-Exupéry wrote that about engineering, but it applies to interfaces just as cleanly. The question is never "what would make this richer?" It's "what would make this clearer?"

Restraint isn't minimalism

This is an important distinction. Minimalism is a visual style — white space, monochrome palettes, sparse type. Restraint is a decision-making posture. You can have a maximalist, dense, richly layered design that is still deeply restrained — every element earning its presence, nothing there by accident or inertia.

The opposite of restraint isn't richness. It's noise. And noise, in a design, is any element that asks for attention without offering anything in return.

Practical discipline

So how do you actually practise restraint when every client wants more features, every stakeholder has one more thing to add, and your own instincts push you toward elaboration?

A few things that have helped:

  • Ask "what job is this doing?" for every element, every time. If you can't answer quickly and clearly, it probably doesn't belong.
  • Default to removing before adding. When something isn't working, try taking something away before layering on a fix.
  • Protect the user's attention. Treat it as a finite, precious resource that your interface is borrowing. Spend it wisely.
  • Sit with discomfort. A screen that feels "too empty" usually just needs time. Give it a week before you fill it.

None of this is easy. The temptation to add is almost gravitational. But the designers and products that last — that people describe as feeling obvious, or just right — they got there through subtraction, not addition. The restraint is the work. And it's worth doing.
